title: Hall effect
description: Detect the presence and polarity of a magnetic field using an analog hall effect sensor.
---

A hall effect sensor detects magnetic fields. Hold a magnet near it, and the sensor responds — the closer the magnet, the more the output value shifts away from its resting state.

Microflow supports **analog** hall effect sensors only.

**Example uses:**
- Detect whether a lid or door with a magnet is open or closed
- Sense the rotation of a shaft or wheel with a magnet attached
- Detect which side of an object is facing down using two magnets of opposite poles

## Output values

The sensor outputs a number on a scale of **0 to 1023**. With no magnet nearby, it reads approximately **512** — the midpoint.

- **North pole nearby** — the value *decreases* (below 512)
- **South pole nearby** — the value *increases* (above 512)
- **No magnet** — the value rests near 512

Because the resting value sits in the middle of the range, you can detect both poles and measure field strength with a single sensor.

<Callout title="Pairing with Map or Compare">
Since the resting value is around 512 (not 0), you will often want to use a [Compare](/docs/microflow-studio/nodes/decide/compare) node to trigger when the value crosses a threshold, or a [Map](/docs/microflow-studio/nodes/shape/map) node to convert the range into something more useful.
</Callout>

## Settings

Open the settings panel to configure:

- **Pin** — which analog pin on your Arduino the sensor is connected to (analog pins are labelled A0, A1, A2, etc.)

## Wiring

Most hall effect sensors have three pins: **5V**, **GND**, and **SIG** (signal).

Not every sensor is wired identically — check the datasheet for your specific model, but the general pattern is:

| Sensor pin | Connect to |
| --- | --- |
| 5V | 5V on your Arduino |
| GND | GND on your Arduino |
| SIG | An analog pin (A0, A1, etc.) |
