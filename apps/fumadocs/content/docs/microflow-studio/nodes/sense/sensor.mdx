If your sensor does not have its own dedicated node in Microflow, you can still use it with the generic Sensor node. You just need to know one thing: is it a **digital** or **analog** sensor?

## Digital vs analog

**Digital sensor** — outputs one of two states: on or off, detected or not detected. These sensors give a clear yes/no answer. Example: a motion detector that says "motion detected" or "no motion."

**Analog sensor** — outputs a continuous range of numbers (typically 0–1023 on an Arduino). These sensors measure a quantity that can vary smoothly. Example: a soil moisture sensor that reads anywhere from dry to saturated.

If you are unsure which type your sensor is, check its datasheet or packaging. A sensor that connects to one of the A0–A5 pins is almost certainly analog. A sensor that only needs a single digital pin is digital.

## Settings

Open the settings panel to configure:

- **Pin** — which pin on your Arduino the sensor is connected to
- **Type** — digital or analog

## When to use this node

Use the generic Sensor node for sensors that do not have a dedicated page in the documentation, such as:

- Flex resistors (bend sensors)
- Soil moisture sensors
- Vibration sensors
- Rain or water level sensors
- Any other standard digital or analog sensor

For sensors that have their own dedicated node — [Button](/docs/microflow-studio/nodes/sense/button), [LDR](/docs/microflow-studio/nodes/sense/ldr), [Force](/docs/microflow-studio/nodes/sense/force), [Hall Effect](/docs/microflow-studio/nodes/sense/hall-effect), [Tilt](/docs/microflow-studio/nodes/sense/tilt), [Potentiometer](/docs/microflow-studio/nodes/sense/potentiometer), and others — use the specific node instead. Dedicated nodes include settings and context specific to that sensor type.
