title: Your first flow
description: Blink the built-in LED on your Arduino in five minutes, with no extra parts.
---

This is your "hello world" with hardware. By the end, the small LED on your Arduino will blink on and off once a second. You will not need any extra components — every supported Arduino board has an LED on pin 13 already soldered to the board.

## What you need

- A computer (Windows, macOS, or Linux)
- A supported Arduino board: Uno, Mega, Leonardo, Micro, Nano, or Yun
- A USB cable that fits your board
- Microflow Studio installed — [install it now](/docs/microflow-studio) if you have not yet

<Callout type="info" title="Template available">
  Studio ships this flow as the **Blink** template — open the **Templates** page and load it to skip the node-by-node setup. Building it by hand is still the best way to learn.
</Callout>

## Step 1 — Plug in your board

Connect the Arduino to your computer with the USB cable. A small power LED on the board should light up. Leave it plugged in for the rest of the tutorial.

## Step 2 — Open Microflow Studio and create a new flow

Launch Microflow Studio. From the home screen, click **New flow**. You will see a blank grid — this is the **canvas**. It is where you build everything.

<Callout type="info" title="Video placeholder">
  10-second clip: opening Studio, creating a new flow, landing on the empty canvas.
</Callout>

## Step 3 — Wait for the board to be detected

In the bottom panel, Studio will say something like *"Waiting for board…"* and then *"Detected: Arduino Uno on COM3"*. The first time you connect a board, it also installs a small program called **StandardFirmata** onto it. This takes 30–60 seconds.

When you see *"Running"*, the board is ready to talk to your flow.

If detection fails, see [Board not detected](/docs/troubleshooting/board-not-detected).

## Step 4 — Add an Interval node

You need something to fire repeatedly. The [Interval](/docs/microflow-studio/nodes/generate/interval) node does exactly that — it emits a tick on a schedule.

1. Open the **Add node** panel (press `n` or click the `+` button).
2. Find **Interval** under the **Generate** category.
3. Drag it onto the canvas.
4. Click the node and set **Interval** to `1000` milliseconds (one second).

## Step 5 — Add an LED node

Now the thing you want to control:

1. Open **Add node** again.
2. Find **LED** under the **Express** category.
3. Drag it onto the canvas.
4. Click the LED node and set **Pin** to `13` — this is the built-in LED on most Arduinos.

## Step 6 — Connect them

Look at the right side of the Interval node. You will see a small dot — this is its **output handle**. Look at the left side of the LED node. You will see another dot — its **input handle**.

Click and drag from the Interval's output to the LED's input. A line appears connecting them. This is an **edge**, and it carries values from one node to the next.

<Callout type="info" title="Video placeholder">
  10-second clip: dragging an edge from one handle to another.
</Callout>

## Step 7 — Watch the LED blink

That's it. The small built-in LED on your Arduino should now toggle on and off once a second.

If it does not, check:

- Is the bottom panel still showing **Running**?
- Did you set the LED's pin to `13`?
- Is the edge actually connected? (it should be a solid line, not faded)

## What just happened?

- The **Interval** node emits a tick every 1000 ms.
- That tick travels along the edge to the **LED** node.
- The LED toggles its pin between high (on) and low (off) on each tick.

Three nodes — Generate → Express — and you have a working hardware project. Every flow you build follows this same pattern: something produces a value, that value flows through other nodes, and eventually something physical happens.
